\section{Problem Statement}
The task of DR grading from fundus photographs is fundamentally an \emph{ordinal} five-class classification problem and the cost of misclassification grows with the distance between the predicted and true grade. Standard image classification models that treat the grades as independent categorical labels therefore discard valuable information and tend to produce clinically implausible errors. 
The problem is further complicated by a severe class imbalance, since the majority of patients exhibit no or mild disease, leaving the higher-severity grades drastically under-represented in available datasets. The objective of this work is to design a convolutional neural network that perform ordinal five-class grading upon classification, mitigates class imbalance, and produces interpretable, clinically aligned predictions evaluated through the Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK) metric and grad-CAM analysis to identify which regions of the image are most important for each prediction.

\subsection{Baseline Model}
We approached the problem the way any standard image classification task would be approached: with an ImageNet-pretrained CNN, a linear head re-mapped to the five DR grades, and a vanilla cross-entropy loss. Three backbones of progressively increasing capacity were chosen so that we could observe how depth and architectural sophistication interact with our small ($\sim$3.6k-image) and heavily imbalanced training set. The first model, used as our reference baseline, is \textbf{ResNet-18} ($\sim$11.2M parameters)---a residual network deep enough to learn rich features but light enough to train end-to-end on a single 4\,GB consumer GPU. The second is \textbf{ResNet-50} ($\sim$25M parameters), chosen to test whether simply going deeper improves performance under identical training conditions. The third is \textbf{EfficientNet-B0} ($\sim$5M parameters), selected as a more parameter-efficient, compound-scaled architecture that often dominates the medical imaging literature for fundus tasks. Across all three runs the optimisation recipe was kept deliberately fixed---Adam optimiser at $\text{lr}=10^{-4}$, $224 \times 224$ input resolution, $10$ training epochs, an $80/20$ stratified train--validation split, and unweighted \textbf{categorical cross-entropy} as the loss function---so that any differences in performance can be traced back to the architecture rather than the optimisation strategy.

\subsection{Baseline Results}
The ResNet-18 baseline achieved a best validation Quadratic Weighted Kappa of \textbf{QWK $= 0.8819$} and accuracy of \textbf{83.49\%}, meeting standard performance benchmarks. However, the core finding from the baseline experiments reveals a critical failure: \textbf{severe overfitting and class imbalance}. The training and validation curves (Figure~\ref{fig:baseline_training_curves}) clearly show the divergence between training and validation loss over 10 epochs, indicating the model is memorising patterns rather than learning generalizable features.

The per-class classification performance (Table~\ref{tab:baseline_classification_report}) exposes the clinical inadequacy of this approach. While Grade~0 (healthy) is recognised almost perfectly (F1-score $=0.95$), the minority and clinically critical grades severely degrade: Grade~3 and Grade~4 show F1-scores of only $0.28$ and $0.34$ respectively. The model fails where it matters most---on advanced disease stages. This pattern confirms that standard categorical cross-entropy loss and naive training are fundamentally unsuited to this ordinal, imbalanced medical imaging task.

\subsection{Defining Objectives}
Through baseline experiments, it is evident that \emph{any} typical image classification CNN, when transplanted directly onto APTOS-2019, fails in the same predictable way: it overfits a small, heavily class-imbalanced medical dataset differ sharply from the natural-image distribution the backbone was pretrained on. The objectives of this project therefore reorient away from chasing yet another architecture and towards directly attacking the root causes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reduce overfitting} through processes like data augmentation, regularisation (dropout, weight decay), and stronger normalisation of the train/validation curves.
    \item \textbf{Fine-tune ResNet and find an appropriate loss function} that better capture the ordinal nature of the problem, such as focal loss and CORAL loss that explicitly models the rank order of the classes.
    \item \textbf{Explore alternatives to flat softmax classification} that respect the ordinal structure and class imbalance of DR severity, including weighted cross-entropy,
\end{enumerate}
