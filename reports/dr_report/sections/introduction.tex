\section{Introduction}
Diabetic Retinopathy (DR) is a microvascular complication of diabetes mellitus and is one of the leading causes of preventable blindness worldwide. The disease progresses silently through five clinically defined severity grades---ranging from no apparent retinopathy (Grade 0), through mild, moderate, and severe non-proliferative DR, to the most advanced proliferative DR (Grade 4). Because the earliest stages are typically asymptomatic, screening through color fundus photography has become the standard first line of defense, allowing ophthalmologists to detect lesions such as microaneurysms, hemorrhages, exudates, and neovascularization before irreversible vision loss occurs. However, manual grading is time-consuming, requires specialist expertise, and suffers from significant inter-observer variability. The growing global burden of diabetes, combined with a shortage of trained graders---particularly in rural and low-resource regions---has motivated the development of automated, deep-learning-based grading systems that can deliver consistent, scalable, and clinically meaningful predictions directly from fundus images.

For this work we use the publicly available \textbf{APTOS 2019 Blindness Detection} dataset, hosted on Kaggle by the Asia Pacific Tele-Ophthalmology Society. It contains $3{,}662$ high-resolution color fundus photographs collected from rural India and labelled by clinicians on the standard five-class International Clinical Diabetic Retinopathy (ICDR) severity scale ($0$--$4$). The dataset is available at \url{https://www.kaggle.com/competitions/aptos2019-blindness-detection}. As supplementary material we additionally reference the \textbf{EyePACS} dataset ($\sim$88{,}000 images, \url{https://www.kaggle.com/c/diabetic-retinopathy-detection}), which is widely used as a large-scale pretraining and benchmarking corpus for DR grading.
